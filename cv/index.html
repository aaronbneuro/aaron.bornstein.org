I research how humans make decisions. I measure brain activity while people make choices, to try to learn what kind of information affects our decisions, how we learn from experience, and how we combine multiple, sometimes conflicting, pieces of information.

Most of what I've done so far focuses on how individual memories -- a single choice we've made in the past -- change our decisions when they come to mind. One of the key findings of this work is that even long-past memories can come back to mind with the slightest nudge, and can bring back a rich set of associated experiences, all of which have a big influence on what we choose in the present. Most recently, I've shown that all of this memory information can also affect how we interpret our visual perceptions -- possibly even change what we see.

This work might have important implications for the treatment of trauma, anxiety disorders, addiction relapse, and the standard approach to modeling economic choices.

More to come!
